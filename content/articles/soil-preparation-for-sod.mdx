## Step 1: Demolition â€” Removing What's Already There

Most sod installations happen on sites with existing vegetation. That existing grass (or weeds, or mixed mess) must come out completely before new sod can go down. Leaving old vegetation beneath new sod creates a layer of decaying organic matter that blocks root contact and breeds disease.

### The Chemical Approach

For existing lawns and heavy weed coverage, herbicide is the most practical first step:

1. Apply glyphosate (Roundup or equivalent) to the entire installation area according to label rates.
2. Wait 10-14 days for complete vegetation death. The lawn should turn completely brown/tan with no green remaining.
3. Do not disturb the dying grass during this periodâ€”cutting or disturbing it reduces herbicide effectiveness.

Glyphosate is non-selective (kills everything it contacts) and has no residual activity (it doesn't persist in soil to harm new grass). Applied correctly, it eliminates existing vegetation without creating barriers to new establishment.

### Physical Removal

After chemical kill (or on bare ground/construction sites), physical removal extracts the dead vegetation:

**Sod cutter method:** A walk-behind sod cutter slices 1-2 inches below the surface, separating the dead grass and its shallow root layer. The dead sod rolls up like carpet and is removed from the site. This is the cleanest approach, leaving smooth soil beneath.

**Rototilling method:** For sites with minimal vegetation or as a second pass after sod cutting, a rototiller churns the top 4-6 inches of soil, incorporating organic residue. The debris decomposes in place rather than being removed.

**Manual method:** For small areas, a square-nose shovel can slice under dead grass and remove sections. This is labor-intensive and impractical for areas larger than a few hundred square feet.

## Step 2: Debris Removal â€” Creating a Clean Canvas

With vegetation removed, the soil surface reveals what's been hidden: rocks, roots, construction materials, and miscellaneous debris that accumulated during earlier activities. All of this must come out.

### Why Debris Matters

**Rocks:** Stones larger than golf ball size create air pockets beneath sod. Where air pockets exist, roots can't make soil contact, and those spots become brown patches.

**Construction debris:** Concrete chunks, nails, lumber scraps, and drywall pieces are common in new construction sites. These don't decompose and create permanent dead zones.

**Buried organic matter:** Chunks of tree roots, old mulch, or prior vegetation create inconsistent soil conditions. Some spots hold moisture; others drain instantly. This inconsistency shows up as uneven growth.

### The Cleanup Process

Walk the entire site methodically, raking debris to the margins for collection. A landscape rake (bow rake) with wide, heavy tines works best for this task. Multiple passes from different directions catch material missed on the first walk.

For sites with extensive debris (new construction is particularly bad), consider mechanical screeningâ€”running soil through a mesh screen to separate rocks and debris. This is labor-intensive but produces excellent results for high-priority projects.

## Step 3: Rough Grading â€” Establishing Drainage Flow

Water must flow away from structures and toward designated drainage areas. This flow path is established during rough gradingâ€”shaping the soil surface to direct water movement.

### The Critical Rules

**Slope away from foundations:** Soil within 10 feet of any structure should slope away at minimum 2% (about 1/4 inch per foot). Steeper is better for drainage, though slopes above 10-15% become erosion concerns.

**No low spots:** Depressions that hold water become perpetual wet zones that drown grass, breed mosquitoes, and damage root systems. Fill low spots now; fixing them after sod is installed is far more difficult.

**Smooth contours:** Avoid creating berms, ridges, or steps in the surface. Water should sheet-flow smoothly toward drainage points, not pool behind minor obstructions.

### Equipment for Grading

Small sites can be rough-graded manually with a landscape rake, moving soil from high spots to low spots. Larger sites require mechanized assistance:

**Skid steer or compact tractor:** Equipment with a grading bucket can move significant soil volumes quickly.

**Power rake (harley rake):** Attachment that pulverizes and grades in a single pass.

**Laser-guided grading:** For premium installations, laser systems ensure precise, consistent grade across large areas.

## Step 4: Soil Testing and Amendment â€” Optimizing the Growing Environment

Jacksonville's native soil is predominantly sandâ€”excellent for drainage but poor for nutrient retention and moisture holding. Understanding your specific soil conditions and amending as needed creates a better environment for root development.

### The Soil Test

Professional soil testing (available through the UF/IFAS Extension or private labs) provides data on:

- **pH level:** Ideal range for most lawn grasses is 6.0-7.0. Jacksonville soils vary but often trend acidic.
- **Nutrient levels:** Phosphorus, potassium, and micronutrient availability.
- **Organic matter content:** How much decomposed material provides structure and water retention.

Testing costs $15-40 and takes 1-2 weeks for results. For production-schedule installations, this timeline must be planned into the project.

### Common Amendments

**For low pH (acidic soil):** Apply pelletized lime according to soil test recommendations. Lime raises pH gradually over 2-3 monthsâ€”it cannot be done as a quick fix.

**For compacted soil:** Core aeration followed by sand or compost topdressing improves structure. This is common in high-traffic areas or clay-based soils (less common in Jacksonville).

**For nutrient-poor soil:** Starter fertilizer (high phosphorus, moderate nitrogen) applied to the surface immediately before sod installation provides accessible nutrients for developing roots.

**For extremely sandy soil:** Incorporation of quality compost at 1-2 inches depth improves water retention and nutrient holding capacity. This is significant work but dramatically improves sand-dominant sites.

## Step 5: Fine Grading â€” The Final Surface

After amendments are incorporated, the surface needs final smoothing. Fine grading creates the flat, consistent plane that sod will contact.

### The Process

1. **Let amended soil settle:** Water the graded surface and let it sit overnight. This reveals any low spots that weren't apparent when soil was loose.

2. **Fill low spots:** Add topsoil to any depressions that appeared after settling.

3. **Rake to final grade:** Using a landscape rake, create a smooth surface with no bumps larger than a golf ball. Rake in perpendicular passes to catch irregularities from different directions.

4. **Roll the surface:** A water-filled lawn roller (about 1/3 full) compresses the fine-graded surface just enough to identify any remaining soft spots without creating compaction. Mark soft spots for additional fill.

5. **Final rake pass:** Lightly scarify the rolled surface with a leaf rake or landscape rake tines. This creates tiny furrows that help sod roots make contact.

### What "Ready for Sod" Looks Like

When soil prep is complete, the surface should be:

- **Smooth:** No bumps or depressions larger than 1 inch variation across 10 feet
- **Firm but not compacted:** Your feet should leave slight impressions but not sink
- **Level with hardscape:** Final grade should be approximately 1 inch below sidewalk, driveway, or patio surfaces. This allows the sod to match finished grade when installed
- **Moist:** Light pre-watering the day before installation ensures sod makes immediate moisture contact

> **âœ… Key Point:** If your heel sinks more than 1 inch when you walk, the soil is too soft. If it feels like concrete, it's too compacted. The sweet spot is firm enough to walk on but soft enough to indent slightly.

## Step 6: Pre-Installation Fertilization

Starter fertilizer applied to the prepared soil surface provides immediately available nutrients right where new roots will develop. This is the ideal fertilization timingâ€”nutrients are in place waiting for roots, rather than applied after roots must seek them.

### Product Selection

Choose a starter fertilizer with a ratio emphasizing phosphorus (the middle number in N-P-K ratios):

- 10-10-10 (balanced)
- 15-30-15 (high phosphorus starter)
- Similar products marketed specifically as "starter fertilizer"

Avoid high-nitrogen formulations (20-0-0, etc.) at installation. Nitrogen promotes leaf growth; you want root growth. High nitrogen before establishment can actually harm new sod.

### Application

Apply at label rates using a broadcast spreader. Lightly rake to incorporate granules into the top 1/4 inch of soil, or water in lightly after application. The fertilizer should be at the soil surface, not buried.

## The Shortcuts and Their Consequences

Every step above has a costâ€”time, equipment, or physical labor. It's tempting to skip steps, especially on projects with budget pressure or time constraints. Here's what happens when you do:

### Skipping Herbicide Kill

Old vegetation beneath new sod doesn't disappear; it decays. This creates a spongy, inconsistent layer that holds excessive moisture in some spots and repels water in others. Disease organisms thrive in the decaying matter. Roots struggle to penetrate through the debris layer.

**Result:** Patchy establishment, fungal disease, and potential complete failure in sections.

### Skipping Debris Removal

Air pockets under sod prevent root-to-soil contact. No contact means no establishmentâ€”the grass directly above debris dies while surrounding areas thrive.

**Result:** Random bare patches that emerge weeks after installation, requiring repair.

### Skipping Grading

Water pools in low spots, drowning grass. Water rushes off high spots, leaving them too dry. The lawn develops permanent wet/dry zones with different grass health, color, and density.

**Result:** Uneven lawn that looks poorly maintained regardless of management.

### Skipping Fertilization

New roots enter nutrient-poor soil. Establishment slows as the grass struggles to find phosphorus for root development. The lawn looks anemic and establishes weakly.

**Result:** Extended establishment period, increased stress vulnerability, poor first-year development.

## FAQ: Soil Prep Questions

**Q: How long does proper soil preparation take?**

A: Allow 2-3 weeks for the complete process. Herbicide requires 10-14 days to fully kill existing vegetation. Soil testing takes 1-2 weeks for results. Physical prep work (grading, debris removal, amendment) typically takes 1-2 days for residential sites.

**Q: Can I prep soil myself to save money?**

A: Small areas (under 1,000 square feet) are manageable with hand tools and rented equipment. Larger sites benefit enormously from professional equipment and experience. The cost difference between DIY and professional prep often closes when equipment rental, disposal fees, and time investment are calculated.

**Q: My contractor says he doesn't need to do all these steps. Is he wrong?**

A: Some contractors shortcut prep to offer lower bids. Ask specifically what prep work is included. If the answer is "we till the existing lawn and lay sod," you're getting minimal prepâ€”and potentially minimal results. Quality installers consider extensive prep as essential, non-negotiable work.

**Q: What if I'm installing sod on bare dirt (new construction)?**

A: New construction sites often have the worst soil conditions: compacted by heavy equipment, contaminated with debris, and stripped of topsoil. Even "bare dirt" needs full prepâ€”sometimes more than established sites.
